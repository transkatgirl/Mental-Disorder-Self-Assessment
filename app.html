Introduction

This application uses existing self-report mental health assessment questionnaires that have been assessed for adequate reliability and validity. However, unlike clinical tools that provide the results from these questionnaires to the mental health professional assessing the individual, this application allows the individual taking the questionnaire to assess their own results.

Allowing individuals to have a better understanding of their own mental health can empower them to make informed decisions regarding their mental health care, and could greatly assist those with limited access to psychiatric services. Although self-report assessments of mental-health have notable limitations (listed below), there still exists evidence (see references section) that there is potential for them to provide useful information with limited bias.

Combining information from multiple sources to maximize the depth and breadth of information available can result in a more accurate picture of an individual's mental health. The results of self-assessments only represent one possible source; it is up to the user to fit them into the bigger picture.

Limitations

Self-report questionnaires contain some inherit limitations in their validity. Individuals can skew their results by exaggerating or under-reporting their symptoms, and can make errors (such as misinterpreting a question or misremembering relevant information) that meaningfully influence the final results. This application does not attempt to assess indicators of potential response biases.

Knowledge of assessment contents can influence how individuals respond to them in the future. This application uses clinical assessments outside of a clinical setting, which may bias future responses to psychological tests. Although this application does make some attempts to prevent the individual from accidentally biasing their results, repeated use of the questionnaire and knowledge of the questionnaire's scoring may bias subsequent responses.

Although the questionnaires used in this application have undergone some validation and are not limited to a specific clinical subpopulation, they may still contain flaws that limit their reliability and validity. Known limitations of the assessments used will be displayed on the results page.

The current version of this application is unable to assess symptoms of developmental disorders (such as Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder or Autism Spectrum Disorder) or substance use disorders. Measures related to these disorders may be added in a future version.

This application is a proof-of-concept and has not been validated for usefulness, accuracy or correctness. This application should be used for educational purposes only and is not a substitute for a diagnosis from a mental health professional. Clinical training is required to make a clinically useful diagnosis, as it is difficult to determine the threshold where symptoms & traits become pathological.

Classifying Mental Disorders

Existing categorical classifications, such as the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) and the International Classification of Diseases (ICD), have significant limitations. They contain significant symptom overlap between disorders, which leads to limited diagnostic reliability and frequent disorder co-occurence. They also enforce arbitrary boundaries between pathology and normality, while current evidence suggests that all mental disorders likely exist on a continuum.

Addressing these limitations requires creating alternate non-categorical mental disorder classification models. However, this is currently an active area of research. Although promising research prototypes exist, none are currently used in widespread clinical practice.

This application currently uses the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) framework. Instead of discrete categories, the HiTOP model uses continuous dimensions of varying levels of specificity, organized hierarchically from narrowest to broadest.

However, many aspects of the current HiTOP model are provisional or incomplete. For example, the current HiTOP model does not cover developmental disorders (such as Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder or Autism Spectrum Disorder). There have been preliminary attempts to create more comprehensive dimensional models, but additional research is needed to confirm their validity.

This application is not associated with or endorsed by any psychiatric organization or research institution of any kind. All references made are done purely to cite relevant research used to create the application.
